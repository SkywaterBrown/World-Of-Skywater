The spiral of a nautilus shell does not follow the Fibonacci sequence. This is the first thing they do not teach you.

What it does follow is a logarithmic spiral, where the growth factor is constant and unhurried. The Fibonacci sequence, by contrast, is a recursive accident—each term the sum of its two predecessors, each ratio converging toward the golden mean like a moth toward flame.

But the approximation is sufficient. Nature does not optimize; it iterates. A leaf positions itself at 137.5 degrees from the last not because it knows the golden angle, but because this arrangement packs the most light into the smallest space.

We see pattern and call it design. We see iteration and call it purpose. The spiral does not care what we call it. It simply grows, chamber by chamber, indifferent to our mythology.
