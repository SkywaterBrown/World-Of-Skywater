The cup is a manifold. The handle, a torus. When you drink, you traverse a homeomorphism between the liquid state and your internal geometry. This is not metaphor.

Consider the crema: a foam topology unstable and ever-changing. Each bubble a minimal surface, each surface a boundary condition of pressure and temperature. We destroy these universes with every sip.

The coffee ring effect leaves its stain on the table—a two-dimensional projection of three-dimensional failure. The evaporation front pins at the contact line, particles accumulate, and we call it accident. But it is physics, relentless and exact.

I have been staring at this cup for three hours. The steam has stopped. The temperature has equilibrated with the room. Thermodynamic death is not dramatic; it is the slow diffusion of heat into the indifferent air.
